Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (5 x 2 = 10 Marks)

1. NOT a characteristic of magnets

Answer: c) Attracts all metals

Magnets only attract magnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt), not all metals like copper or aluminum.

2. Powerhouse of the cell

Answer: b) Mitochondria

Mitochondria produce energy (ATP) through cellular respiration, hence called the powerhouse.

3. Separating salt from seawater

Answer: c) Evaporation

Water evaporates leaving behind salt crystals. This is how sea salt is produced.

4. Organ that pumps blood

Answer: c) Heart

The heart is a muscular organ that pumps blood throughout the body continuously.

5. Light travels in

Answer: b) Straight lines

This property is called rectilinear propagation of light.

Section B: Short Answer Questions (5 x 2 = 10 Marks)

1. Define element with examples

Element: A pure substance made up of only one type of atoms that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.

Metallic elements: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Gold (Au), Silver (Ag)

Non-metallic elements: Oxygen (O), Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N), Sulphur (S)

2. Difference between breathing and respiration

Breathing: A physical process of inhaling oxygen and exhaling carbon dioxide. It involves the lungs, diaphragm, and ribs.

Respiration: A chemical process where food (glucose) is broken down in cells using oxygen to release energy. The equation is:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy

3. Electromagnet vs Permanent magnet

Differences:

• Electromagnet works only when electric current flows; permanent magnet works always

• Electromagnet's strength can be increased; permanent magnet's strength is fixed

• Electromagnet can be switched on/off; permanent magnet cannot

Uses of electromagnets: Electric bells, cranes for lifting heavy iron objects, MRI machines, speakers

4. Components of blood and their functions

1. Red Blood Cells (RBC): Carry oxygen from lungs to body cells and carbon dioxide back to lungs

2. White Blood Cells (WBC): Fight against germs and infections; provide immunity

3. Platelets: Help in blood clotting to stop bleeding from wounds

4. Plasma: Liquid part that carries nutrients, hormones, and waste products

5. Image in mirror vs rough wall

Plane mirror: Has a smooth, polished surface that causes regular reflection. Light rays reflect at the same angle, forming a clear image.

Rough wall: Has an uneven surface causing irregular/diffused reflection. Light rays scatter in different directions, so no clear image is formed.

Section C: Long Answer Questions (5 x 4 = 20 Marks)

1. The Cell - Structure and Function:

a) Definition and discovery:

A cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. It is the smallest unit of life that can perform all life processes.

Robert Hooke discovered cells in 1665 while observing a thin slice of cork under a microscope.

b) Organelles found only in plant cells:

1. Cell wall - Provides rigidity and protection

2. Chloroplasts - Contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis

3. Large central vacuole - Stores water and maintains turgidity

c) Functions:

(i) Nucleus: Controls all cell activities; contains genetic material (DNA); directs protein synthesis

(ii) Cell membrane: Controls entry and exit of substances; protects the cell; semipermeable barrier

(iii) Vacuole: Stores water, nutrients, and waste; maintains cell turgidity in plants

d) Diagram: Student should draw a plant cell showing: Cell wall, Cell membrane, Nucleus, Chloroplast, Vacuole, Cytoplasm, Mitochondria (at least 5 labeled parts)

2. Magnetism:

a) Magnetic field:

The region around a magnet where its magnetic force can be experienced is called the magnetic field.

To show magnetic field exists: Sprinkle iron filings on a paper placed over a bar magnet and tap gently. The iron filings arrange themselves in curved lines from one pole to another, showing the magnetic field lines.

b) Two methods of making a magnet:

1. Single touch method: Stroke an iron bar repeatedly with one pole of a magnet in the same direction. The bar becomes magnetized with the end where stroking ends becoming opposite to the stroking pole.

2. Electrical method: Wind an insulated copper wire around an iron bar and pass electric current through it. The iron bar becomes an electromagnet. Remove current and it retains some magnetism as a permanent magnet.

c) Breaking a bar magnet:

When a bar magnet is broken into two pieces, each piece becomes a complete magnet with its own north and south poles. You cannot isolate a single pole - this shows that magnetic poles always exist in pairs.

d) Three ways of demagnetization:

1. Heating: Heating a magnet to high temperature destroys its magnetism

2. Hammering: Repeated hammering disturbs the alignment of magnetic domains

3. Electrical method: Passing alternating current (AC) through a coil around the magnet demagnetizes it

3. Elements, Compounds and Mixtures:

a) Differences between compound and mixture:

Compound Mixture
Elements combine in fixed proportion Components mix in any proportion
Properties differ from constituent elements Components retain their original properties
Can only be separated by chemical methods Can be separated by physical methods

b) Classification:

(i) Air - Mixture (contains nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, etc.)

(ii) Water - Compound (H₂O - hydrogen and oxygen in fixed ratio 2:1)

(iii) Iron - Element (contains only iron atoms)

(iv) Sugar - Compound (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ - carbon, hydrogen, oxygen)

c) Symbols:

Oxygen - O, Nitrogen - N, Carbon - C, Calcium - Ca, Sodium - Na

d) Sublimation:

Sublimation is the process where a solid directly changes to gas without passing through the liquid state, and the gas directly changes back to solid on cooling.

Example: Separating ammonium chloride from a mixture of ammonium chloride and salt. When heated, ammonium chloride sublimes (turns to gas) and collects on a cool surface as solid, while salt remains behind.

4. The Circulatory System:

a) Four chambers of the heart:

1. Right Atrium - Receives deoxygenated blood from body

2. Right Ventricle - Pumps deoxygenated blood to lungs

3. Left Atrium - Receives oxygenated blood from lungs

4. Left Ventricle - Pumps oxygenated blood to body

Left side receives oxygenated blood (from lungs via pulmonary veins)

b) Differences between arteries and veins:

Arteries Veins
Carry blood away from heart Carry blood towards heart
Have thick, elastic walls Have thin walls
No valves (except in aorta) Have valves to prevent backflow

c) Blood groups:

The four blood groups are: A, B, AB, and O

O is the universal donor because it has no antigens on red blood cells, so it can be given to people of any blood group without causing clumping/rejection.

d) Blood pressure and healthy practices:

Blood pressure: The force exerted by blood on the walls of arteries as the heart pumps blood. It is measured in mmHg and expressed as systolic/diastolic (e.g., 120/80 mmHg).

Healthy practices:

• Regular exercise (at least 30 minutes daily)

• Eating a balanced diet low in salt and fat

• Maintaining healthy body weight

• Avoiding smoking and excessive stress

5. Light:

a) Rectilinear propagation of light:

Light travels in straight lines. This property is called rectilinear propagation of light.

Example: When sunlight enters a dark room through a small hole, we can see a straight beam of light. Dust particles in the path show the straight path of light. Also, formation of shadows proves light travels in straight lines.

b) Shadow formation:

When an opaque object is placed in the path of light, it blocks the light rays. The dark region formed behind the object where light cannot reach is called a shadow.

Conditions for shadow formation:

1. A source of light

2. An opaque object to block light

3. A screen to receive the shadow

c) Pinhole camera:

A pinhole camera is a simple camera without a lens. It consists of a dark box with a tiny hole (pinhole) on one side and a translucent screen on the opposite side.

Working: Light from an object passes through the pinhole in straight lines. Light from the top of the object reaches the bottom of the screen, and light from the bottom reaches the top, forming an inverted image.

Diagram: [Box with pinhole on left, screen on right, showing light rays crossing through the hole to form inverted image]

d) Why shadows are always dark:

A shadow is formed in the region where light cannot reach because it is blocked by an opaque object. Since there is no light in the shadow region (or very little light), the shadow appears dark. The color of the object does not matter because the shadow is simply an absence of light, not a reflection of the object's color.
