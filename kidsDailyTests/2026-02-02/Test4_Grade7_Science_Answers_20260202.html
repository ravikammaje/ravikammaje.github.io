Section B: Short Answer Questions (5 x 2 = 10 Marks)

1. Two laws of reflection Physics

First Law: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

Second Law: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r).

2. Differences between elements and compounds Chemistry

Element Compound
Made of only one type of atom Made of two or more different types of atoms chemically combined
Cannot be broken down into simpler substances Can be broken down into elements by chemical methods

3. Parts of human brain and function of cerebrum Biology

Three parts of the brain:

1. Cerebrum (forebrain) 2. Cerebellum (hindbrain) 3. Medulla oblongata (brain stem)

Function of cerebrum: The cerebrum controls thinking, memory, intelligence, reasoning, learning, emotions, and voluntary actions. It receives sensory information and controls speech, vision, and hearing.

4. Physical change vs Chemical change Chemistry

Physical change: A change in which no new substance is formed. It is usually reversible.

Example: Melting of ice, dissolving sugar in water

Chemical change: A change in which a new substance is formed with different properties. It is usually irreversible.

Example: Burning of paper, rusting of iron

5. Global warming and greenhouse gases Geography

Global warming: Global warming is the gradual increase in the average temperature of Earth's atmosphere due to the greenhouse effect caused by increased levels of greenhouse gases.

Greenhouse gases:

1. Carbon dioxide (CO2)

2. Methane (CH4)

3. Nitrous oxide (N2O)

4. Water vapor

Section C: Long Answer Questions (5 x 4 = 20 Marks)

1. Light and Reflection: Physics

a) Definitions:

(i) Incident ray: The ray of light that falls on a surface.

(ii) Reflected ray: The ray of light that bounces back from the surface.

(iii) Normal: An imaginary line perpendicular to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence.

(iv) Angle of incidence: The angle between the incident ray and the normal.

b) Diagram: The diagram should show:

- A plane mirror (represented as a straight line with cross-hatching)

- Normal (perpendicular to mirror at point of incidence)

- Incident ray (approaching the mirror)

- Reflected ray (bouncing off the mirror)

- Angle of incidence (∠i) and angle of reflection (∠r) marked

c) Characteristics of image in plane mirror:

1. Image is virtual (cannot be captured on a screen)

2. Image is erect (upright)

3. Image is the same size as the object

4. Image is laterally inverted (left appears as right)

5. Image is as far behind the mirror as the object is in front

d) Angle of reflection:

Angle between incident ray and mirror = 35°

Angle of incidence = 90° - 35° = 55°

By law of reflection, angle of reflection = 55°

2. Elements, Compounds and Mixtures: Chemistry

a) Definitions:

(i) Element: A pure substance made up of only one type of atom that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. Examples: Oxygen, Iron, Gold

(ii) Compound: A pure substance made up of two or more different elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio. Examples: Water (H2O), Salt (NaCl)

(iii) Mixture: A substance made by physically mixing two or more substances without any chemical reaction. The components retain their properties. Examples: Air, Salt solution

b) Classification:

- Oxygen: Element

- Salt (NaCl): Compound

- Air: Mixture

- Iron: Element

- Sugar solution: Mixture

- Carbon dioxide: Compound

c) Chromatography:

Chromatography is a technique used to separate the components of a mixture based on their different rates of movement through a stationary phase (paper or gel) when carried by a moving solvent.

Use: Separating colors in ink, identifying substances in forensic science, testing food colors.

d) Separating sand, salt, and iron filings:

Step 1: Use a magnet to remove iron filings (iron is magnetic).

Step 2: Add water to the remaining mixture. Salt dissolves in water; sand doesn't.

Step 3: Use filtration to separate sand (residue) from salt solution (filtrate).

Step 4: Use evaporation to get salt crystals from the salt solution.

3. Human Excretory System: Biology

a) Excretion and excretory organs:

Excretion: The process of removing metabolic waste products from the body.

Excretory organs and their waste products:

1. Kidneys: Urea, excess water, salts (as urine)

2. Lungs: Carbon dioxide and water vapor

3. Skin: Sweat (water, salts, small amounts of urea)

4. Large intestine: Undigested food (feces)

b) Diagram of urinary system:

The diagram should show and label:

- Two kidneys (bean-shaped, on either side of spine)

- Two ureters (tubes connecting kidneys to bladder)

- Urinary bladder (muscular sac that stores urine)

- Urethra (tube that carries urine out of the body)

c) Structure and function of kidney:

Structure: Bean-shaped organs, about 10-12 cm long. Each kidney has an outer cortex and inner medulla. Contains about 1 million filtering units called nephrons.

Function:

- Filters blood to remove waste products (urea, excess salts, water)

- Maintains water and salt balance in the body

- Produces urine which is stored in the bladder

d) Dialysis:

Dialysis is an artificial method of removing waste products and excess water from the blood when the kidneys fail to function properly.

When required: When both kidneys fail (kidney failure or end-stage renal disease) and cannot filter blood properly.

4. Physical and Chemical Changes: Chemistry

a) Characteristics:

Physical Change:

1. No new substance is formed

2. Usually reversible

3. Only physical properties change

4. No change in mass or composition

Chemical Change:

1. New substance(s) with different properties is formed

2. Usually irreversible

3. Chemical properties change

4. May involve heat, light, gas, or color change

b) Classification:

(i) Burning of wood: Chemical change

(ii) Melting of ice: Physical change

(iii) Digestion of food: Chemical change

(iv) Dissolving sugar in water: Physical change

(v) Cooking an egg: Chemical change

c) Iron nails in copper sulphate solution:

When iron nails are kept in copper sulphate solution, the iron displaces copper. The blue color fades, and reddish-brown copper deposits on the iron nail.

Fe + CuSO4 → FeSO4 + Cu

This is a chemical change because a new substance (iron sulphate and copper) is formed.

d) Reversible and Irreversible changes:

Reversible: Melting of ice, Dissolving salt in water, Stretching a rubber band, Folding paper

Irreversible: Burning of paper, Cooking food, Ripening of fruit, Rusting of iron

5. Human Nervous System: Biology

a) Nervous system:

The nervous system is a network of specialized cells (neurons) that coordinates actions and transmits signals between different parts of the body.

Two main parts:

1. Central Nervous System (CNS): Brain and spinal cord

2. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Nerves that connect CNS to the rest of the body

b) Diagram of a neuron:

The diagram should show and label:

- Cell body (contains nucleus)

- Dendrites (short, branched extensions that receive signals)

- Axon (long fiber that transmits signals away from cell body)

- Nerve endings/terminals (at the end of axon)

- Myelin sheath (insulating layer around axon) - optional

c) Reflex actions:

Reflex actions are quick, automatic, involuntary responses to stimuli that do not involve thinking. They are controlled by the spinal cord, not the brain.

Examples:

1. Withdrawing hand when touching a hot object

2. Blinking when something comes near the eye

3. Sneezing when dust enters the nose

4. Knee-jerk reflex when knee is tapped

d) Reflex arc when touching hot object:

1. Stimulus: Hot object touches skin (receptor)

2. Sensory neuron: Carries signal from skin to spinal cord

3. Relay neuron: In spinal cord, passes signal to motor neuron

4. Motor neuron: Carries signal to muscles in arm

5. Response: Muscles contract, hand is pulled away

This entire process happens in a fraction of a second, before you consciously feel the pain.
