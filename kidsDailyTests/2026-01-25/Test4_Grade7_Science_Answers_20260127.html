Atom: The smallest particle of an element that cannot be divided further and retains the properties of the element. Atoms cannot exist independently (except noble gases).
Example: Hydrogen atom (H), Oxygen atom (O)

Molecule: The smallest particle of an element or compound that can exist independently and is made up of two or more atoms.
Example: Oxygen molecule (O₂), Water molecule (H₂O)

Weather: The atmospheric conditions (temperature, humidity, rainfall, wind) at a particular place and time. It changes from day to day or even hour to hour.
Example: Today it is sunny and warm.

10. A thermos flask keeps liquids hot by preventing all three methods of heat transfer:

1. Conduction: Prevented by the vacuum (empty space) between the double walls. Since there is no medium, heat cannot transfer by conduction.

2. Convection: Also prevented by the vacuum, as convection requires a medium (air/liquid) to transfer heat.

3. Radiation: Prevented by the silvered/shiny inner walls which reflect heat radiation back into the liquid.

Award marks for mentioning vacuum (1 mark) and silvered walls/reflection (1 mark).

11. Three methods of heat transfer:

1. Conduction:
- Heat transfer through direct contact between particles
- Occurs in solids (especially metals)
- Requires a medium
- Example: A metal spoon becoming hot when placed in hot tea; heat traveling through an iron rod when one end is heated

2. Convection:
- Heat transfer through the movement of fluids (liquids or gases)
- Occurs when heated particles move from hot region to cold region
- Requires a medium
- Example: Water boiling in a pot (hot water rises, cold water sinks); sea breeze and land breeze; heating a room with a heater

3. Radiation:
- Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves
- Does NOT require a medium (can travel through vacuum)
- Travels at the speed of light
- Example: Heat from the Sun reaching Earth; feeling warmth near a fire even without touching it

Answer to question: Radiation does NOT require a medium for heat transfer.

Award marks: Each method with explanation and example (1 mark each = 3 marks), Identifying radiation as not requiring medium (1 mark)
12. Symbols and Valencies:

a) Sodium: Symbol = Na, Valency = 1
b) Chlorine: Symbol = Cl, Valency = 1
c) Calcium: Symbol = Ca, Valency = 2
d) Oxygen: Symbol = O, Valency = 2

Chemical Formulae:

(i) Sodium chloride:
Na (valency 1) + Cl (valency 1)
By cross-multiplication: Na₁Cl₁ = NaCl

(ii) Calcium oxide:
Ca (valency 2) + O (valency 2)
By cross-multiplication: Ca₂O₂ = CaO (simplified by dividing by 2)

13. Difference between Meristematic and Permanent Tissue:

MERISTEMATIC TISSUE:

Characteristics:
1. Cells are actively dividing and growing
2. Cells are small, thin-walled with dense cytoplasm
3. No intercellular spaces between cells
4. Cells have large nucleus and no vacuoles

Location:
- Apical meristem: At tips of roots and shoots (for growth in length)
- Lateral meristem: In stems and roots (for growth in thickness)
- Intercalary meristem: At the base of leaves or internodes

Function: Responsible for growth of the plant

PERMANENT TISSUE:

Characteristics:
1. Cells have lost the ability to divide
2. Cells are large with thick walls
3. May have intercellular spaces
4. Cells may have vacuoles

Function: Provide support, protection, and perform various functions like storage, photosynthesis, transport

Types: Simple permanent tissue (parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma) and Complex permanent tissue (xylem, phloem)

Award marks: Two characteristics each (2 marks), Location of meristematic tissue (1 mark), Functions (1 mark)
14. Structure of a Neuron (Nerve Cell):

Students should draw a neuron with these labeled parts:

Three main parts:

1. Cell Body (Cyton):
- Contains nucleus and cytoplasm
- Function: Controls all activities of the nerve cell and processes information

2. Dendrites (Dendrons):
- Short, branched projections from the cell body
- Function: Receive signals/impulses from other neurons or sense organs and transmit them to the cell body

3. Axon:
- Long, single fiber extending from the cell body
- Often covered with myelin sheath (insulating layer)
- Ends in axon terminals (nerve endings)
- Function: Transmits nerve impulses away from the cell body to other neurons, muscles, or glands

Additional parts in diagram:
- Myelin sheath: Insulating covering on axon
- Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps in myelin sheath
- Axon terminals: Endings that connect with other cells

Award marks: Correct diagram (1 mark), Three labeled parts (1.5 marks), Functions of each part (1.5 marks)
15. Properties of Water:

a) Three states of water:

1. Solid (Ice): Below 0°C (freezing point)
- Water freezes at 0°C to form ice

2. Liquid (Water): Between 0°C and 100°C
- Water exists as liquid at normal room temperature

3. Gas (Water vapor/Steam): Above 100°C (boiling point)
- Water boils at 100°C to form steam/water vapor

Note: These temperatures are at normal atmospheric pressure (1 atm or 760 mm Hg)

b) Water as a universal solvent:
Water is called a universal solvent because it can dissolve more substances than any other liquid. Most ionic compounds, salts, sugars, acids, bases, and many gases dissolve in water. This property makes water essential for life, as it helps transport nutrients and minerals in living organisms.

c) Examples:
- Substance that dissolves: Sugar, salt (sodium chloride), potassium permanganate, copper sulfate
- Substance that does NOT dissolve: Sand, oil, chalk powder, wood

Award marks: States with temperatures (1.5 marks), Universal solvent explanation (1.5 marks), Examples (1 mark)
