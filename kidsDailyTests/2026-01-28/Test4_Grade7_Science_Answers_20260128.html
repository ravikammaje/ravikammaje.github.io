Answer: b) Reflection

Answer: b) Chlorophyll

Chlorophyll absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis

Section B: Short Answer Questions (5 × 2 = 10 Marks)

1. Two laws of reflection: Physics

Law 1: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r)

Law 2: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

2. Physical vs Chemical change: Chemistry

Physical Change:

- No new substance is formed

- Usually reversible

- Example: Melting of ice, evaporation of water

Chemical Change:

- New substance is formed

- Usually irreversible

- Example: Burning of wood, rusting of iron

3. Word equation for photosynthesis: Biology

Carbon dioxide + Water → (sunlight/chlorophyll) → Glucose + Oxygen

Or: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → (sunlight/chlorophyll) → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Gas released: Oxygen (O₂)

4. Properties of metals and non-metals: Chemistry

Properties of Metals:

1. Good conductors of heat and electricity

2. Malleable (can be beaten into sheets) and ductile (can be drawn into wires)

Properties of Non-Metals:

1. Poor conductors of heat and electricity (insulators)

2. Brittle (break when hammered)

5. Human nervous system: Biology

Three main parts:

1. Brain

2. Spinal cord

3. Nerves

Function of spinal cord:

- Carries messages between the brain and the rest of the body

- Controls reflex actions

Section C: Long Answer Questions (5 × 4 = 20 Marks)

1. Light and Reflection: Physics

a) Diagram should show:

- Plane mirror (vertical line)

- Incident ray (arrow pointing toward mirror)

- Reflected ray (arrow pointing away from mirror)

- Normal (perpendicular dotted line to mirror at point of incidence)

- Angle of incidence (∠i) between incident ray and normal

- Angle of reflection (∠r) between reflected ray and normal

b) Angle of reflection:

If angle of incidence = 35°, then angle of reflection = 35°

(By the first law of reflection: ∠i = ∠r)

c) Why mirror shows image but wall doesn't:

A plane mirror has a smooth, polished surface that causes regular reflection - all light rays reflect in the same direction, forming a clear image.

A rough wall has an uneven surface causing diffuse/irregular reflection - light rays scatter in all directions, so no clear image is formed.

d) Speed of light in vacuum:

3 × 10⁸ m/s (or 300,000 km/s)

2. Physical and Chemical Changes: Chemistry

a) Classification:

(i) Rusting of iron - Chemical change

(ii) Boiling of water - Physical change

(iii) Digestion of food - Chemical change

(iv) Cutting of wood - Physical change

b) Heating sugar until black:

This is a chemical change (caramelization/charring).

When sugar is heated excessively, it breaks down and forms carbon (black residue) and water vapor. New substances are formed, and the original sugar cannot be recovered.

c) Reversibility of chemical changes:

Chemical changes are generally irreversible.

Example: Once paper is burned, we cannot get the original paper back from the ash.

(Some chemical changes can be reversed by another chemical reaction, but not easily)

d) Indicators of chemical change:

1. Change in color

2. Release of gas (bubbles)

(Also: Change in smell, release of heat/light, formation of precipitate)

3. Photosynthesis: Biology

a) Definition and equation:

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants make their own food (glucose) using carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight in the presence of chlorophyll.

6CO₂ + 6H₂O → (sunlight/chlorophyll) → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

b) Four factors necessary:

1. Sunlight

2. Chlorophyll

3. Carbon dioxide

4. Water

c) Where photosynthesis occurs:

Photosynthesis mainly takes place in the mesophyll cells of the leaf, specifically in the chloroplasts.

This is because chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, and leaves have many chloroplasts to maximize photosynthesis.

d) Importance of photosynthesis:

1. Produces oxygen that all living organisms need to breathe

2. Produces food (glucose) that is the source of energy for all living things (either directly or indirectly through food chains)

4. Metals and Non-Metals: Chemistry

a) Good conductors of electricity:

Copper, Silver, Gold, Aluminum

Best conductor: Silver (followed by copper)

b) Corrosion:

Corrosion is the slow deterioration of metals when they react with air, moisture, and chemicals in the environment.

Example: Rusting of iron (iron reacts with oxygen and moisture to form iron oxide - rust)

Prevention methods: Painting, oiling/greasing, galvanizing (coating with zinc), electroplating

c) Why cooking utensils are made of metals:

1. Metals are good conductors of heat - they transfer heat quickly and evenly to food

2. Metals have high melting points - they don't melt at cooking temperatures

3. Metals like aluminum and steel are strong, durable, and don't react with food

d) Non-metals:

Solid non-metals: Carbon (graphite, diamond), Sulfur, Phosphorus

Gaseous non-metal: Nitrogen, Hydrogen, Chlorine

Essential for breathing: Oxygen

5. Atmosphere and Global Warming: Geography

a) Five layers of atmosphere (lowest to highest):

1. Troposphere (0-12 km)

2. Stratosphere (12-50 km) - contains the ozone layer

3. Mesosphere (50-80 km)

4. Thermosphere (80-700 km)

5. Exosphere (above 700 km)

b) Greenhouse effect:

The greenhouse effect is the trapping of heat by certain gases in the atmosphere. These gases allow sunlight to enter but prevent heat from escaping, warming the Earth like a greenhouse.

Greenhouse gases: Carbon dioxide (CO₂), Methane (CH₄), Water vapor, Nitrous oxide

c) Global warming:

Global warming is the gradual increase in Earth's average temperature due to increased greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.

Causes: 1. Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) 2. Deforestation

Effects: 1. Melting of polar ice caps and glaciers 2. Rising sea levels, flooding coastal areas

d) Ways to reduce global warming:

1. Plant more trees (they absorb CO₂)

2. Use renewable energy (solar, wind) instead of fossil fuels

(Also: Reduce, reuse, recycle; use public transport; energy-efficient appliances)
