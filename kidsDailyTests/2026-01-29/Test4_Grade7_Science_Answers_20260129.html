Section B: Short Answer Questions (5 x 2 = 10 Marks)

1. Define: (a) Frequency of sound (b) Amplitude of sound Physics [2]

Answer:

(a) Frequency: The number of vibrations (oscillations) produced per second. It is measured in Hertz (Hz). Frequency determines the pitch of sound - higher frequency means higher pitch.

(b) Amplitude: The maximum displacement of a vibrating particle from its mean position. It determines the loudness of sound - greater amplitude means louder sound.

2. Valency of Na and O, and formula of their compound Chemistry [2]

Answer:

(a) Valency of Sodium (Na) = 1

(b) Valency of Oxygen (O) = 2

Formula: Na2O (Sodium oxide)

Working: Cross-multiply valencies - Na has valency 1, O has valency 2, so 2 Na atoms combine with 1 O atom.

3. Difference between meristematic and permanent tissue Biology [2]

Answer:

Meristematic tissue:

- Cells are actively dividing

- Cells are small with thin walls

- Responsible for growth

- Example: Root tip, shoot tip

Permanent tissue:

- Cells have stopped dividing

- Cells are fully differentiated

- Perform specific functions

- Example: Xylem, Phloem, Parenchyma

4. Why does a thermos flask keep liquids hot or cold? Physics [2]

Answer:

A thermos flask has a double-walled glass container with a vacuum between the walls.

1. Vacuum prevents conduction and convection - heat cannot transfer through empty space by these methods.

2. Silvered walls prevent radiation - the shiny surface reflects heat radiation back inside (or outside for cold liquids).

3. Cork/plastic stopper prevents heat loss from the top.

This minimizes all three modes of heat transfer, keeping liquids at their original temperature.

5. Three types of rocks with examples Geography [2]

Answer:

1. Igneous rocks - formed from cooled magma/lava

Example: Granite, Basalt

2. Sedimentary rocks - formed from deposited sediments over time

Example: Sandstone, Limestone, Shale

3. Metamorphic rocks - formed from existing rocks changed by heat and pressure

Example: Marble (from limestone), Slate (from shale)

Section C: Long Answer Questions (5 x 4 = 20 Marks)

1. Sound and its properties Physics [4]

a) What is sound? How is it produced?

Sound is a form of energy that produces the sensation of hearing. It is produced by vibrations of objects. When an object vibrates, it creates disturbances in the surrounding medium (air, water, or solid), causing compressions and rarefactions that travel as longitudinal waves.

b) Why sound cannot travel through vacuum:

Sound requires a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel. It propagates through the vibration of particles in the medium. In a vacuum, there are no particles to vibrate, so sound cannot travel. This is why astronauts cannot hear each other directly in space.

c) What is an echo? Conditions for hearing echo:

An echo is the repetition of sound caused by reflection of sound waves from a hard surface.

Conditions for hearing an echo:

- The minimum distance between the source and reflecting surface should be at least 17.2 meters (at 20Â°C)

- The reflecting surface should be large and hard

- The time gap between original sound and echo should be at least 0.1 seconds

d) Distance calculation:

Time = 5 seconds

Speed of sound = 340 m/s

Distance = Speed x Time

Distance = 340 x 5 = 1700 meters

The thunderstorm is 1700 m (or 1.7 km) away.

2. Atomic Structure and Valency Chemistry [4]

a) What is an atom? Subatomic particles:

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction while retaining the chemical properties of that element.

Three subatomic particles:

1. Proton - positive charge (+1), found in nucleus

2. Neutron - no charge (neutral), found in nucleus

3. Electron - negative charge (-1), orbits around nucleus

b) Valency definition:

Valency is the combining capacity of an atom. It is the number of electrons an atom can lose, gain, or share to complete its outermost shell (achieve a stable configuration of 8 electrons, or 2 for the first shell).

Valency is determined by the number of electrons in the outermost shell of an atom.

c) Symbols and valencies:

- Calcium (Ca) - Valency 2

- Chlorine (Cl) - Valency 1

- Nitrogen (N) - Valency 3

- Sulphur (S) - Valency 2

d) Chemical formulae:

(i) Calcium chloride: Ca has valency 2, Cl has valency 1

Formula: CaCl2

(ii) Aluminium oxide: Al has valency 3, O has valency 2

Formula: Al2O3

3. Plant and Animal Tissues Biology [4]

a) What is a tissue?

A tissue is a group of cells that are similar in structure and work together to perform a specific function.

Importance:

- Division of labor in the body

- Efficient functioning of organs

- Specialized cells for specialized functions

b) Four types of animal tissues and their functions:

1. Epithelial tissue - Covers body surfaces and lines internal organs; provides protection

2. Connective tissue - Connects and supports different parts of the body; examples: blood, bone, cartilage

3. Muscular tissue - Enables movement through contraction and relaxation

4. Nervous tissue - Transmits nerve impulses; controls and coordinates body activities

c) Xylem tissue:

Structure: Xylem consists of dead cells with thick, lignified walls. It includes vessels, tracheids, xylem fibers, and xylem parenchyma.

Function:

- Transports water and dissolved minerals from roots to leaves (upward)

- Provides mechanical support to the plant

d) Voluntary vs Involuntary muscles:

Voluntary muscles:

- Under conscious control

- Also called skeletal/striated muscles

- Example: Muscles of arms, legs

Involuntary muscles:

- Not under conscious control

- Also called smooth muscles

- Example: Muscles of stomach, intestines

4. Heat Transfer Physics [4]

a) Three modes of heat transfer:

Conduction: Transfer of heat through a material without the actual movement of particles. Heat passes from particle to particle. Occurs mainly in solids.

Convection: Transfer of heat through the actual movement of heated particles (fluid flow). Occurs in liquids and gases.

Radiation: Transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves without requiring any medium. Can occur through vacuum.

b) Metal utensils with wooden/plastic handles:

- Metal (body): Good conductor of heat - allows quick and uniform heating of food

- Wood/Plastic (handle): Poor conductors (insulators) - prevents heat from reaching our hands, avoiding burns

This combination allows efficient cooking while maintaining safety.

c) Sea breeze and land breeze:

Sea Breeze (Day):

- During day, land heats up faster than sea

- Hot air over land rises (convection)

- Cooler air from sea moves toward land to replace it

- This creates a breeze from sea to land

Land Breeze (Night):

- At night, land cools faster than sea

- Warm air over sea rises

- Cooler air from land moves toward sea

- This creates a breeze from land to sea

d) Examples of heat transfer by radiation:

1. Heat from the Sun reaching Earth through space

2. Heat from a fire reaching us without touching it

(Also: Heat from a room heater, Heat felt near a hot bulb)

5. Weathering and Rock Formation Geography [4]

a) Weathering and its types:

Weathering is the breaking down of rocks into smaller pieces by natural agents like water, wind, temperature changes, and living organisms.

Three types:

1. Physical/Mechanical weathering - Breaking of rocks by physical forces (temperature changes, frost action, wind)

2. Chemical weathering - Decomposition of rocks by chemical reactions (oxidation, carbonation, hydration)

3. Biological weathering - Breaking of rocks by living organisms (plant roots, burrowing animals)

b) Formation of igneous rocks:

Igneous rocks are formed when hot molten magma from inside the Earth cools and solidifies.

- Intrusive igneous rocks: Form when magma cools slowly inside Earth (large crystals). Example: Granite

- Extrusive igneous rocks: Form when lava cools quickly on Earth's surface (small or no crystals). Example: Basalt

Examples: Granite, Basalt, Pumice, Obsidian

c) Sedimentary vs Metamorphic rocks:

Sedimentary rocks:

- Formed from layers of sediments deposited over millions of years

- Often contain fossils

- Layered structure

- Example: Sandstone, Limestone

Metamorphic rocks:

- Formed when existing rocks change due to intense heat and pressure

- Usually harder than original rock

- May have wavy bands or layers

- Example: Marble (from limestone), Slate (from shale)

d) Importance and methods of soil conservation:

Importance:

- Soil is essential for growing crops and food production

- Takes thousands of years to form; once lost, difficult to replace

- Supports plant life and ecosystems

Methods:

1. Terrace farming - Creating steps on slopes to reduce water runoff

2. Afforestation - Planting trees to hold soil with roots

(Also: Contour plowing, Strip cropping, Windbreaks, Crop rotation)
